Section 02: Material Hermeneutics of Machine Guns and Battlefield Reality

01 Entities

01.1 Human (H)

  • American infantry soldiers in World War II.

01.2 Technology (T)

  • Machine guns, representative of the broader technological shift in warfare.

01.3 World (W)

  • The battlefield, characterized by danger, chaos, and psychological stress.

02 Mapping Interactions

02.1 Use

  • Soldiers use machine guns (and other weapons) to engage the enemy, striving to achieve fire superiority.
  • "The correct use of weapons by a formation in battle comes of the perfecting of controls over men who are physically beyond reach."
  • Disconnect between technical operation and effective deployment in combat.

02.2 Mediation

  • Machine guns transform the battlefield into a realm of overwhelming firepower and impersonal violence.
  • Enemy becomes largely invisible, increasing the psychological impact of unseen threats.
  • "The enemy fires. The transition of that moment is wholly abnormal. He had expected to see action. He sees nothing. There is nothing to be seen."
  • Shifts focus from direct confrontation to psychological effects of unseen firepower.

02.3 Interpretation

  • Mediated experience clashes with soldier's initial interpretations shaped by training and societal expectations.
  • Training emphasizes individual marksmanship and heroism; reality highlights collective action and psychological resilience.
  • "The heart of the matter is to relate the man to his fellow soldier... when the ground offers him no comfort and weapons fail."
  • Suggests misinterpretation needs correction through training that accounts for psychological impact of technology.

03 Highlighting Dynamics

03.1 Intermediary Role

  • Machine gun technology acts as a powerful intermediary, shaping the relationship between soldiers and the battlefield.
  • Creates a disconnect between expectation of visible enemies and reality of fighting an unseen, technologically empowered force.
  • Influences soldier's psychological state, impacting willingness to engage and effectiveness of actions.
  • "The challenge for military training then becomes accounting for this intermediary role of technology and preparing soldiers for the psychological realities of technologically mediated warfare."
